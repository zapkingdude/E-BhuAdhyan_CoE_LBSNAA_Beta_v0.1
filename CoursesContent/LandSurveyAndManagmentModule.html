Welcome to Chapter 1 : Introduction to Land Surveying - Key Concepts

Basics of land surveying and its methods.

Introduction

Surveying is the art of making measurements of the relative positions of points on the surface of the earth and drawing them to a scale on paper to show the natural and artificial features in their correct horizontal and vertical relationship.

Land surveying is a time-honored profession that continues to be essential in the modern world. It is crucial for defining property boundaries and supporting civil engineering projects, making it a fundamental part of land development and management.

What is Land Surveying?

Land surveying involves the precise measurement and mapping of land to create an accurate representation of a property or area. This information is used for various purposes, including construction projects, resolving boundary disputes, and land development. Businesses, government agencies, and individuals often hire land surveyors to perform these essential tasks.

Types of Land Surveying
  • Boundary Surveying:
    Determines property lines and corners based on deeds and previous surveys. It provides a legal property description commonly used in land transactions. A mortgage survey is a simplified version, primarily identifying buildings on a property.
  • Topographic Surveying:
    Maps the natural and man-made features of the land, including elevation changes, which are essential for engineering, planning, and design purposes.
  • Construction Surveying:
    Ensures structures are built accurately by marking reference points that align with engineering plans.
  • Cadastral Surveying:
    Establishes land ownership boundaries, often required for taxation or legal purposes.
  • Geodetic Surveying:
    Measures extensive land areas while accounting for Earth's curvature. It utilizes satellite and aerial imaging for large-scale mapping.
  • Hydrographic Surveying:
    Focuses on mapping water bodies, including depth, tides, and underwater features, crucial for navigation and offshore construction projects.

Key Terms In Land Surveying

  • Benchmark:
    A designated reference point with a known elevation, used in surveys for vertical measurements. Benchmarks help establish consistent elevation data across projects.
  • Contour Line:
    Lines on a topographic map that connect points of equal elevation. Walking along a contour line means maintaining a constant elevation, making these lines useful for visualizing terrain shape and slope.
  • Datum Point:
    A fixed reference point or surface from which elevations or coordinates are measured. Sea level is a common datum used for height measurements in surveying.
  • Elevation:
    The height of a point above a specified level, usually measured relative to sea level. Elevation plays a crucial role in both flat and hilly terrains.
  • Triangulation:
    A surveying technique that determines the position of a point by measuring angles from known reference points. Though widely used historically, modern tools like GPS and total stations have largely replaced this method.
  • Plat:
    A detailed map that outlines a property's boundary lines, dimensions, and features. Plats are commonly required for legal and permit purposes.
  • Right-of-Way:
    A legal right granting passage through another person’s property, often used for roads, utility lines, or public access pathways.
  • Azimuth:
    A horizontal angle measured clockwise from a reference direction (usually north) to a specified point. This measurement is essential for navigation and precise land mapping.
  • Metes and Bounds:
    A land description method using physical features, distances, and angles to define property boundaries. "Metes" refer to specific measured distances, while "bounds" describe natural or artificial boundary markers.

Surveying Methods

The surveying methods are broadly classifed as two methods, Plane Survey & Geodetic Survey

  • Plane Survey
    • Considers surface of the earth as Horizontal plane
    • Limited to smaller area
    • The curvature of the Earth is not considered into computations.
    • Ex: Old Cadastral, Urban planning Surveys
  • Geodetic Survey
    • Considers surface of the earth as Spherical
    • Smaller to larger areas
    • The curvature of the Earth is considered into computations.
    • Ex: All modern surveys treats earth as spherical

This transformation of the 3D elements to 2D plane is done by projection of the 3D plane by using several mathemathical model based on the model of Earth.     • A datum provides a frame of reference for measuring locations on the surface of the earth. It defines the origin and orientation of latitude and longitude lines
    • First Nationwide National Spatial Reference Frame (NGRF) established during 19th century through the Great Trigonometric Survey.

  • Map Projections
    • A map projection is a set of mathematical transformations, which are used to represent the Earth’s curved surface on a flat map.

Tools Used In Land Surveying

As mentioned in the beginning, Land surveying involves the precise measurement and mapping of land to create an accurate representation of a property or area. Hence, to achieve the accuracy and precision in the surveying process, there are many tools used for measuring the land. These tools can be separated as:

  • Legacy/Traditional Surveying Tools
  • Modern Surveying Tools

Surveying technologies are used to measure, map, and analyze the physical world, creating accurate representations of land, buildings, and other infrastructure. The evolution of surveying technologies has seen a dramatic shift from manual, labor-intensive methods to highly automated, precise, and digital technolgical systems.

Each and every tools has their own Pro's and Cons, the judgement of choosing the neccessary tools for a particular survey job mostly rely on few factors as metioned below:

  • Type of terrains where survey needs to be done
  • Total area needed to be covered under the survey
  • Time constraints
  • Budget constraints
  • Skilled manpower availability for driving the survey based on the selected surveying technology
As the above mentioned scenarios occur, a surveyor might choose one type of surveying tools or mix of several types together to expedite the work more fastly. Following are the types of Surveying tools:

  • Traditional Surveying Tools:
    Chain Surveying :
    • Pro : Simple, inexpensive, and quick for small areas.
    • Con : Limited accuracy, affected by terrain, requires manual labour, and not suitable for large areas.
    Compass Surveying :
    • Pro : Suitable for rough terrain, easy to carry and use.
    • Con : Low accuracy, affected by magnetic interference, and less reliable for large-scale mapping.
    Plane Table Surveying :
    • Pro : Good for rough areas, quick for mapping small regions.
    • Con : Time-consuming for large areas, requires a skilled operator, and accuracy depends on the operator's skill.
    Theodolite Surveying :
    • Pro : High accuracy in measuring angles, more precise than earlier methods.
    • Con : Requires a steady hand and experience, prone to errors if not handled carefully.
    Tacheometry Surveying :
    • Pro : Faster than traditional methods, suitable for rough terrain and large areas.
    • Con : Requires specialized equipment and training, lower accuracy than modern techniques.
  • Modern Surveying Tools
    Photogrammetry Surveying :
    • Pro : Can survey large areas quickly, effective for mapping inaccessible areas using aerial photos.
    • Con : Expensive, requires skilled operators and specialized equipment
    GPS/GNSS Surveying :
    • Pro : High accuracy, works globally, fast, and requires minimal human intervention.
    • Con : Expensive equipment, affected by signal obstructions (e.g., tall buildings or trees), Not suitable for detail survey.
    Electronic Total Station (ETS) :
    • Pro : Provides highly accurate measurements of both angles and distances, integrates with digital systems for easier data processing.
    • Con : Expensive, requires regular calibration, and is affected by line-of-sight obstructions.
    LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) :
    • Pro : Highly accurate, can capture vast amounts of data in 3D, effective in challenging terrain and for topographic surveys.
    • Con : Expensive equipment, data processing can be complex and time-consuming.
    UAV (Drone) Surveying :
    • Pro : Can cover small size areas quickly, provides high-resolution imagery, and is cost-effective for some applications.
    • Con : Limited by flight time and weather conditions, requires regulatory approvals in certain areas.
    Remote Sensing Surveying :
    • Pro : Can capture data over wide areas, useful for monitoring and mapping land cover, vegetation, and environmental changes. It’s non-intrusive and can access difficult-to-reach areas.
    • Con : Expensive equipment, and lack of fine resolution data that needed for Cadastral Surveying and large-scale detailed surveying.
    Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) GNSS Surveying :
    • Pro : Provides centimeter-level accuracy, ideal for precision surveying in the field.
    • Con : Expensive, requires a base station, and limited by signal obstructions and satellite availability.
    Network Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) GNSS Surveying :
    • Pro : No need of base station, provides centimeter-level accuracy, ideal for precision surveying in the field.
    • Con : Expensive, requires network of base stations, and limited by signal obstructions.
    Network Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) GNSS Surveying :
    • Pro : No need of base station, provides centimeter-level accuracy, ideal for precision surveying in the field.
    • Con : Expensive, requires network of base stations, and limited by signal obstructions.

Welcome to Chapter 2 : Survey of India’s CORS Service

Survey of India's CORS service which helps to generate high accuracy and precise points using rovers and NRTK system.

Introduction

CORS (Continuously Operating Reference Station) is a NRTK system of permanent, high-precision GNSS stations operated by Survey of India that continuously collect data and are used to provide real-time corrections for other GNSS receivers in India.

The Survey of India (SOI) has established a Continuously Operating Reference System (CORS) across India as part of the National Geodetic Reference Frame. Currently, SOI has set up 1,047 permanent CORS reference stations and is actively working on increasing their density. Additionally, as part of the NAKSHA initiative, SOI is collaborating with the Department of Land Resources (DoLR), Government of India, to further expand the CORS network, including both temporary and permanent stations in towns and urban local bodies (ULBs).

CORS provides a real-time positioning service for precise GNSS observations accessible to the general public. With CORS, users can obtain high-precision position data within 3–5 minutes, achieving an accuracy of 2–3 cm. Previously, accurate point positioning relied on SOI’s Ground Control Points (GCPs), which required 2–3 days to determine precise locations.

CORS is an integrated network of permanent GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System) base stations that deliver high-precision positioning data in real-time or post-processed modes. Each reference station is equipped with a high-precision GNSS receiver and antenna, continuously collecting satellite signals from constellations such as GPS, GLONASS, Galileo, and BeiDou.

The collected data undergoes processing to generate corrections that mitigate errors caused by atmospheric interference, satellite orbit inaccuracies, and clock deviations. The network then transmits these corrections to GNSS rover units via the internet, radio, or cellular networks. Users connect to the network through specialized software or hardware to apply these corrections, significantly enhancing their positional accuracy.

Welcome to Chapter 3 : Field Survey Steps - An Overview

A field survey is a systematic process used to gather accurate spatial and geographical data for mapping, land assessment, and infrastructure planning.
It involves several key steps to ensure precision and reliability.

1. Introduction

A field survey is an essential process in land management, urban planning, and construction, where data is gathered directly from the site. It involves systematic steps to ensure accurate measurements, reliable data collection, and precise mapping. By following these steps, surveyors can efficiently document spatial information, analyze terrain, and create detailed reports for decision-making. Below is an overview of the key steps involved in a field survey.

  • Planning & Preparation: Define objectives, choose equipment, and review existing data.
  • Reconnaissance (Recce): Visit the site to identify key landmarks and obstacles.
  • Establishing Control Points: Set up reference points for accurate measurements.
  • Data Collection: Measure distances, angles, and elevations using survey instruments.
  • Data Recording & Documentation: Log observations and measurements systematically.
  • Data Processing & Analysis: Convert raw data into maps, reports, and GIS databases.
  • Validation & Quality Check: Verify accuracy and make necessary corrections.
  • Final Reporting & Mapping: Present processed data in the form of maps or charts.

2. Field Survey of Land Parcel - Basics

A field survey of a land parcel is a crucial process in land management, real estate, and urban planning. It involves systematically measuring and mapping a specific land area to establish accurate boundaries, topographical features, and ownership details. The data collected through field surveys helps in legal documentation, infrastructure development, and decision-making.

2.1 Key Steps in a Land Parcel Survey
2.1.1. Pre-Survey Preparation
  • Define survey objectives (boundary identification, land division, etc.).
  • Gather land records, maps, and relevant documents.
  • Select appropriate survey equipment (GPS, total station, etc.).
2.1.2. Site Reconnaissance
  • Visit the site to assess topography and obstacles.
  • Mark key reference points for measurements.
2.1.3. Boundary Identification
  • Locate legal boundaries and verify records.
  • Mark boundary points with stakes or markers.
2.1.4. Measurement & Data Collection
  • Use GNSS, theodolite, or total station to measure distances and angles.
  • Record GPS coordinates and survey data.
2.1.5. Data Processing & Reporting
  • Analyze and validate collected data.
  • Generate maps, reports, and cadastral records.

Land parcel surveys are essential for accurate land ownership records, preventing disputes, and facilitating infrastructure planning. Modern technology like GNSS, GIS, and drones has improved survey efficiency and accuracy.

3. Tools Used for Land Survey

All field survey activities can be carried out by using field devices like survey grade GNSS Receivers (also known as GNSS Rovers) in NRTK/Static Mode integrated with/without field data collector connected to Central Database through an enterprise GIS Server in online/offline mode, ETS GNSS etc. depending on the terrain conditions and availability of the instruments.

3.1. Understanding GNSS Receivers (GNSS Rovers)

A GNSS receiver, also known as a GNSS rover, is a device that helps determine precise locations on Earth. It is commonly used in land surveying, farming, construction, and mapping.

These receivers use signals from satellites in space to figure out their position. Some well-known satellite systems are:

  • GPS (USA)
  • NavIC (India) – India's own satellite navigation system, developed by ISRO for precise positioning in the Indian region.
  • GLONASS (Russia)
  • Galileo (Europe)
  • BeiDou (China)

To calculate an accurate location, a GNSS rover needs signals from at least four satellites. The device measures how long it takes for each satellite signal to reach it, then uses that information to find its position on the ground.

3.1.1 Why GNSS Needs Corrections?

If you use a GNSS device on its own, the location it gives may not be completely accurate. This happens because of various errors, such as:

  • Satellite timing issues
  • Delays caused by the atmosphere
  • Signal interference from buildings or trees

These errors can make the location several meters off, which is not good for tasks that require high precision, like construction or land surveys.

3.1.2 How We Improve Accuracy?

To get precise locations, GNSS rovers use correction data from special reference stations. There are two types:

  • CORS Network - These are SOI stations that continuously send correction data in real-time
  • GCPs (Ground Control Points) - Fixed locations with known coordinates that help adjust GNSS measurements.
3.1.3 How It Works in Simple Terms?

Imagine you're measuring a straight road with a regular tape measure, but the tape stretches and shrinks slightly due to heat. To get the exact length, you need a standard reference to compare against.

CORS and GCPs act like this standard reference for GNSS, helping remove errors and making location data super precise – down to a few centimeters!

With these corrections, GNSS receivers become much more reliable for surveying, mapping, and other applications that require accurate positioning.

3.1.4 How GNSS Rovers Work?

A GNSS Rover is a high-tech device used to find precise locations on Earth. It works by receiving signals from multiple satellite systems like GPS (USA), GLONASS (Russia), Galileo (Europe), BeiDou (China), and NavIC (India).

Key Components of a GNSS Rover:

  • GNSS Receiver – The core part that processes satellite signals to determine location.
  • High-Performance Antenna – Ensures strong and reliable signal reception, even in cities or dense forests.
  • Data Collector – Stores and processes location data, often running software for real-time mapping.
  • Communication Module – Connects to CORS correction services through radio or mobile networks, improving accuracy to the centimeter level using Network RTK (NRTK).
  • Durable Casing – Designed to withstand harsh weather and tough field conditions.

3.2. Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) GPS – High-Precision Positioning

RTK GPS is an advanced positioning system that improves standard GPS accuracy to centimeter-level precision. It works by using correction data from a reference station.

3.2.1 How RTK GPS Works:?
  • Reference Station - A fixed GPS receiver at a known location continuously tracks satellite signals and calculates corrections.
  • Rover Unit – A mobile GPS receiver (GNSS Rover) collects satellite signals and receives real-time corrections from the reference station.
  • Real-Time Corrections - The rover applies these corrections, significantly improving positioning accuracy.

This system is widely used in land surveying, construction, precision farming, and mapping, where highly accurate location data is essential.

3.3. Electronic Total Station (ETS) with GNSS – Advanced Surveying Technology

Electronic Total Station (ETS) with GNSS combines two powerful surveying technologies to improve accuracy and efficiency in land measurement and mapping.

3.3.1 How it Works?
  • Electronic Total Station (ETS) - Measures angles (horizontal & vertical) and distances using laser or infrared technology.
  • GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System) – Provides precise positional data by receiving signals from satellites.
  • Integrated System - Combining both technologies allows surveyors to achieve high-precision measurements over long distances, even in challenging terrains where one system alone may not be sufficient.

This hybrid system is widely used in land surveying, construction, infrastructure projects, and geospatial data collection, ensuring accurate and reliable results.

3.4. Field Data Collector (FDC) with WebGIS for Field Survey

A Field Data Collector (FDC) is a specialized device or system used for collecting and recording GIS data directly from the field. It enables surveyors to capture, update, and manage spatial and attribute data efficiently.

3.4.1 Key Features of FDC
  • Rugged Tablet or Device - Works seamlessly with GNSS Receivers (GNSS Rovers) or Electronic Total Stations (ETS) for accurate data collection.
  • Basemap Visualization & Feature Extraction – llows surveyors to view maps and mark boundary points, land parcels, and other features in real-time.
  • Attribute Data Collection - Supports the linking of descriptive information (e.g., landowner details, land use, etc.) with spatial data.
  • WebGIS & Central Database Integration -
    • Field devices connect to a central database through an Enterprise GIS Server, enabling real-time data transmission.
    • Enhances efficiency in land management and ensures end-to-end digital workflow.
  • CORS Network Integration - Ensures high survey accuracy by receiving correction signals through a survey-grade GNSS Receiver.

This system significantly improves land surveying, cadastral mapping, and infrastructure planning, ensuring precise and up-to-date geographic data.

3.5. Distance-Distance Measurement using a Laser Range Finder
3.5.1 What it is?
  • A Laser Range Finder is a device that uses laser beams to measure distances accurately.
3.5.2 How it works?
  • It sends a laser beam to a target, and when the beam bounces back, the device calculates the exact distance.
3.5.3 Why it is used in land surveys?
  • Helps measure distances between points like property corners, slopes, and terrain features.
  • Useful when a point is not visible on the map.
3.5.4 How surveyors use it?
  • They stand at two well-known points.
  • Measure the distance from both points to the unknown location.
  • The exact position is then determined on a digital map using special software.

This method helps accurately map land features without needing to physically reach every point.

3.6. Reference Stations – Correcting GPS/GNSS Errors

GPS/GNSS receivers can have errors due to satellite clock issues, atmospheric delays, receiver noise, and signal interference. These errors make location measurements inaccurate.

To fix this, GPS/GNSS data needs to be corrected using Reference Stations, which are fixed locations with accurately known coordinates. These stations provide correction data to improve accuracy.

3.6.1 What it is?

There are two types of Reference Stations:

  • CORS Network (Continuous Operating Reference Stations) - A network of active stations providing real-time corrections (Refer previous chapter "Survey of India's CORS Service" in this module to read more about this CORS stations).
  • GCPs (Ground Control Points) - Fixed passive reference points used for post-processing corrections.

4. Field survey - Methodologies and Salient Instructions

For an example, let’s take a survey scenario of urban local body properties and plots.

4.1. Surveying Properties in a City – Simplified Steps
1.1 Gather Information:
  • Before starting, the survey team collects property documents, maps, tax records, and other legal details from the local government.
  • If there is an online city survey system (Web-GIS), it can be used to make the process smoother.
1.2. Prepare Maps & Tools:
  • Existing maps and layout plans should be aligned correctly with real-world locations.
  • The team carries printed and digital maps on specialized field devices (FDCs) or rugged laptops with GPS software.
  • If GPS is not available, high-precision survey equipment (GNSS Rovers, Total Stations) is used to mark property boundaries.
1.3. Field Survey Process:
  • Existing maps and layout plans should be aligned correctly with real-world locations.
  • The team carries printed and digital maps on specialized field devices (FDCs) or rugged laptops with GPS software.
  • If GPS is not available, high-precision survey equipment (GNSS Rovers, Total Stations) is used to mark property boundaries.
1.4. Measuring Boundaries:
  • If physical markers like fences exist, the center of these structures is considered the boundary unless proven otherwise.
  • The team takes photos of properties with high-resolution camera, and ensure that it is geo-tagged.
1.5. Using GPS & Other Tools:
  • After identifying boundaries, the team uses GNSS rovers (high-precision GPS) to measure the property accurately.
  • If boundaries are unclear on maps, they are measured directly on the ground using GPS or other survey tools.
  • In areas where GPS doesn’t work well (due to tall buildings, trees, or power lines), measurements are taken using alternative tools like Electronic Total Stations or measuring tapes.
1.6.Finalizing Property Data:
  • The collected data is processed and drawn on GIS software to create digital property maps.
  • Each land parcel is assigned a temporary number until verified by local officials.
  • A final property ID is given after all approvals.

This ensures that all properties are properly recorded, mapped, and linked to government records for better city planning and management.

4.2. Field Survey: Salient Instructions
  • Survey team goes to the field with the basemap and identifies as well as documents boundary markers or any physical landmarks on the ground and same should be correlated with basemap.
  • Ground team to visit each plot or land parcel to verify urban land boundaries. Boundary points are to be noted by the ground truthing team using GNSS Rover, in presence of land owners, and neighbors. Field survey team to also ground validate the features extracted from the ORI such as utilities, buildings etc.
  • Collection of the plot details available with the survey team shall be tallied with the details contained in the attribute documents available with the land holders, which could include Record of Right, Sale Deed, Lease Deed, Gift Deed, Relinquishment Deed, Settlement Deed, Occupancy Certificate, Deed of Partition, Lay out Plan, Property Tax Receipt, Building Sanction Plan, Will, General Power of Attorney (GPA), Agreement to Sell(ATS), Utility Bills, Mutation Certificate, presented by the land holder in ownership possession of the plot/land parcel.

    • Surveyors visit the site with all necessary equipment.
    • The GNSS Rover is placed at key boundary points to collect precise coordinates.
    • If any points are missing from maps, measurements are taken using the Laser Range Finder.
  • Creating the Digital Property Map:
    • Recorded GPS coordinates are uploaded to GIS software on the rugged laptop.
    • A property boundary layer is created in real-time.
    • Surveyors verify the accuracy and make corrections if necessary.
  • Finalizing the Survey:
    • The boundary data is reviewed and validated.
    • The updated property boundaries are officially recorded.
    • The final digital property map is submitted for approval.

This method ensures high-accuracy property mapping, minimizing errors and disputes

4.3.3. Electronic Total Stations in combination with GNSS:
  • Purpose of the Survey:
    • To accurately mark property boundaries using Electronic Total Stations (ETS) and GNSS.
    • This method ensures precise boundary mapping, even in areas where GNSS signals are weak.
  • Essential Equipment:
    • GNSS Instrument / CORS – Provides accurate reference points.
    • Electronic Total Station (ETS) – Measures angles and distances with high precision.
    • Rugged Laptop with GIS Softwar – Plots boundary data in real-time.
    • Laser Range Finder – Measures distances where GPS signals are weak.
    • Printed Basemap – A hardcopy reference of property layouts which helps to compare new and old boundary data.
    • Trained Surveyors – Skilled surveyors to operate the equipment.
  • Field Survey Process:
    • Step 1: Establish at least two Ground Control Points (GCPs) using GNSS Receivers (Rovers)
    • Step 2: Align the Electronic Total Station using these GCPs.
    • Step 3: Use the Total Station to record the exact coordinates of property boundary points.
    • Step 4: If any points are missing from existing maps, measure them using the Laser Range Finder.
  • Creating the Digital Property Map:
    • The recorded coordinates are uploaded to GIS software on a rugged laptop.
    • A property boundary layer is created and visualized in real-time.
    • Any necessary corrections are made based on on-ground observations.
  • Finalizing the Survey:
    • The boundary data is verified for accuracy.
    • The updated property boundaries are officially recorded..
    • The final digital property map is submitted for approval.

This method ensures high-accuracy property mapping, even in dense urban areas or regions with weak GNSS signals.

4.3.4. Equipment Preparation:
  • GNSS Rover Setup – Ensure the GNSS receiver is functional, charged, and updated withthe latest firmware.
  • Field Data Collector (FDC) – Install survey software (e.g., Trimble Access) and check Bluetooth, GPRS and Wi-Fi functionalities.
  • Power Backup – Carry spare batteries and portable chargers.
  • Connectivity – Confirm reliable internet access for the CORS network.
4.3.5. Define Survey Parameters:
  • Coordinate System – Use WGS-84 or a geographical projection system.
  • Survey Style – Configure GNSS receiver settings for RTK surveying.
  • Signal Tracking – Enable GNSS constellations (GPS, GLONASS, Galileo, NavIC, etc.).
  • Elevation Mask – Set to 10–15 degrees to avoid low-angle satellites.
  • PDOP Mask – Set the threshold to <3 for high accuracy.
  • Verify NTRIP – credentials (IP address, port, username, and password).
4.3.6. Survey Setup:
  • Mount the GNSS receiver on a tripod or pole and position it vertically using the bubblelevel.
  • Power on the receiver and check for satellite lock (at least 5 satellites for a 3D fix).
4.3.7. Establish Connection to CORS:
  • Primary ways to establish communication between the rover and CORS networks viainternet Networked Transport of RTCM via Internet Protocol (NTRIP). This method allowsthe rover to connect to the CORS network through the internet.
  • Obtain NTRIP Access Information: Contact the CORS provider or use a public NTRIPservice. You will need an NTRIP caster URL, a username, and password (if required), andthe specific mount point of the CORS station.
  • Configure the Rover: In the rover’s software or receiver settings, configure the NTRIPclient by entering the caster URL, mountpoint, and login credentials.
  • Establish Connection: The rover will connect to the CORS network via the internet,receive RTK corrections, and apply them in real-time for improved accuracy.
  • Monitor the Connection: Ensure that the rover has an active connection to the CORS network and that it is receiving correction data
4.3.8. Pair the GNSS receiver with the Field Data Collector via Bluetooth:

Open Bluetooth settings and pair with the GNSS receiver (default PIN: 0000). Connect to the CORS network:

  • Enter the IP address and port of the CORS server.
  • Input NTRIP credentials (username and password)
  • Verify real-time corrections are active.
4.3.8. Establish Base Point (if required):
  • If a known control point is available, position the GNSS receiver over it.
  • Measure the point using RTK corrections to establish the reference for the survey.
4.3.8. Land Parcel Survey Execution - Boundary Point Measurement:
  • Step 1: Get Ready -
    • Go to the first boundary point of the property.
    • Set up the GNSS Rover and make sure it's stable.
  • Step 2: Accurate Positioning -
    • Hold the survey pole straight (use the bubble level).
    • Keep the antenna upright for a clear satellite signal.
    • Enter the antenna height into the field device.
    • Ensure a clear view of the sky for accurate measurements.
  • Step 3: Measuring the Boundary Points -
    • Start measuring and wait for the device to confirm the position.
    • Walk to each corner of the property and record the coordinates (latitude, longitude, elevation).
    • For shared boundaries (with neighboring properties), measure only once to avoid differences.
    Image Depicting Path Followed For Collection of Property Boundary Points Using GPS Rovers
  • Step 4: Special Cases – Corner Properties & Roadside Land -
    • If a property is at a road intersection, measure all road-facing sides carefully.
    • If a boundary overlaps with a building, review and verify with a supervisor.
    • Locate and record the roadside boundary points with high precision.
  • Step 5: Checking for Accuracy -
    • Compare new measurements with old maps — adjust if the difference is more than 5 cm.
    • Ignore small differences (less than 10 cm) but correct any major variation (over 50 cm).
    • If a measurement places a boundary over a building (wrong position), it must be rechecked and corrected.
  • Step 6: Handling Difficult Areas -
    • Use offset measurements for blocked points (e.g., behind walls).
    • Re-measure if accuracy limits are not met.
  • Step 7: Finalizing the Survey -
    • Calculate the total area of the property in the survey software.
    • Add details like owner’s name, survey number, and tax details in the attributes collector fields.
    • Take a photo of the property’s front view and make sure its geotagged.
    • Move to the next property and repeat the processes.
4.3.9. Post-Survey Tasks - Data Export:
  • Export the collected data from the Field Data Collector to the system
  • Format: CSV, DXF, or SHP (depending on project requirements).
  • Projection: UTM projection with datum WGS-84 or local coordinate system.
  • Save data securely in multiple locations (e.g., cloud storage, USB drives) and make sure there are ample backups of the collected data.
4.3.10. Surveyed Data Processing:
  • Import data into GIS or CAD software for analysis and mapping.
  • Process RTK and raw data in Trimble Business Center (TBC) or equivalent software.
  • Perform baseline processing and network adjustment.
  • Verify data consistency and accuracy.
4.3.11. Metadata Creation, Parcel Layoutand Report Generation:
  • Prepare detailed maps showing parcel boundaries, coordinates and area of the land parcel metadata.
  • Include metadata.
  • Measurement accuracy (horizontal and vertical).
  • Coordinate system and datum.
  • CORS network details and reference points used.
  • Area of the land parcel.
4.3.12. Best Practices:
  • Equipment Maintenance: Regularly calibrate GNSS receivers and ensure proper storage.
  • Signal Monitoring: Avoid surveying during high PDOP conditions or satellite outages.
  • Safety Protocols: Follow safety guidelines in remote or hazardous survey areas.
  • Backup the data: Survey team must ensure that survey data is stored in proper devices.
