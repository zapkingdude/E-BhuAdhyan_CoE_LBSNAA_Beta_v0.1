Welcome to Chapter 1 : Introduction to NAKSHA Programme - Key Concepts

The NAKSHA program aims to create a comprehensive and accurate geospatial database for urban land records. By integration aerial and field surveys with advanced GIS technology, the program enhances efficiency in land governance, streamlines property ownership records, and facilitates urban planning. Accurate geospatial data ensures improved decision-making, efficient land use planning and smoothen and certain property transactions.

Introduction

The NAKSHA Programme is designed to support urban, peri-urban and regionalplanning by generating and providing high-quality, geospatial data to theStates/UTs. It is essential to prioritize the creation of land records in urban and peri-urban areas to enhance the ease of living for citizens, streamline the urban landrecords optimization and improve overall urban development outcomes, includingbetter property tax collection, urban planning and redevelopment. It would also leadto ease of business by way of easier access to credits, precise geospatial decisions,and ownership clarity etc. A key step in this direction is the creation of the urbanland record system which provides comprehensive and reliable information for allurban land parcels within ULBs and peri-urban areas in an easily accessible format.

Given the limitations of existing datasets, the most reliable approach to establishingsuch a system is to conduct a survey and create a database using the latesttechnology. This process should include robust safe guards by cross-referencingexisting data bases and actively involving citizens through ground truthing and atransparent process for addressing objections. This approach ensures accuracy,reliability and public trust in the new urban land record system.

NAKSHA - A City Survey Program

Rationale for NAKSHA

NAKSHA aims to create and modernize urban land records, ensuring transparency, efficiency, effectiveness and economic growth.
  • LACK OF PROPER URBAN LAND RECORD MAINTENANCE:
    Most urban areas, except in a few states like Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Gujarat and Goa, have outdated or unstructured land records, causing inefficiencies in governance and taxation
  • RAPID URBANIZATION & EXPANSION:
    Both horizontal and vertical growth in urban and peri-urban areas demand a robust and updated land record system.
  • HIGH LAND VALUE & KEY ASSET FOR CITIZENS:
    With land being the most valuable asset for many, precise and transparent records are crucial for security and financial stability.
  • OWNERSHIP DISPUTES AND LENGTHY LEGAL BATTLES:
    Missing or unclear urban land records cause disputes, prolonged court cases, and ownership uncertainties.
  • OBSTACLE TO PLANNED INFRASTRUCTURE DEVELOPMENT:
    Unclear land titles hinder urban planning, delaying key infrastructure projects, increasing costs.
  • DIFFICULTIES IN PROPERTY TRANSACTIONS & CREDIT ACCESS:
    Poor land documentation complicates property deals and limits citizens' ability to secure loans.
  • LACK OF TRANSPARENCY, ACCESSIBILITY, AND ACCURACY:
    Weak urban land record systems enable fraud, corruption and tax evasion, reducing public trust.

Key Terms In Land Surveying

  • Benchmark:
    A designated reference point with a known elevation, used in surveys for vertical measurements. Benchmarks help establish consistent elevation data across projects.
  • Contour Line:
    Lines on a topographic map that connect points of equal elevation. Walking along a contour line means maintaining a constant elevation, making these lines useful for visualizing terrain shape and slope.
  • Datum Point:
    A fixed reference point or surface from which elevations or coordinates are measured. Sea level is a common datum used for height measurements in surveying.
  • Elevation:
    The height of a point above a specified level, usually measured relative to sea level. Elevation plays a crucial role in both flat and hilly terrains.
  • Triangulation:
    A surveying technique that determines the position of a point by measuring angles from known reference points. Though widely used historically, modern tools like GPS and total stations have largely replaced this method.
  • Plat:
    A detailed map that outlines a property's boundary lines, dimensions, and features. Plats are commonly required for legal and permit purposes.
  • Right-of-Way:
    A legal right granting passage through another person’s property, often used for roads, utility lines, or public access pathways.
  • Azimuth:
    A horizontal angle measured clockwise from a reference direction (usually north) to a specified point. This measurement is essential for navigation and precise land mapping.
  • Metes and Bounds:
    A land description method using physical features, distances, and angles to define property boundaries. "Metes" refer to specific measured distances, while "bounds" describe natural or artificial boundary markers.

Surveying Methods

The surveying methods are broadly classifed as two methods, Plane Survey & Geodetic Survey

  • Plane Survey
    • Considers surface of the earth as Horizontal plane
    • Limited to smaller area
    • The curvature of the Earth is not considered into computations.
    • Ex: Old Cadastral, Urban planning Surveys
  • Geodetic Survey
    • Considers surface of the earth as Spherical
    • Smaller to larger areas
    • The curvature of the Earth is considered into computations.
    • Ex: All modern surveys treats earth as spherical

Introduction To Projection Systems

The maps are basically the representation of the 3D real world elements into a 2D plane. This transformation of the 3D elements to 2D plane is done by projection of the 3D plane by using several mathemathical model based on the model of Earth. Following are the types of Earth Model and their corresponding projections used for mapping.

  • Ellipsoid and Datum:
    • Ellipsoid approximates the shape of the earth; a datum defines the position of the ellipsoid relative to the center of the earth.
    • A datum provides a frame of reference for measuring locations on the surface of the earth. It defines the origin and orientation of latitude and longitude lines
    • First Nationwide National Spatial Reference Frame (NGRF) established during 19th century through the Great Trigonometric Survey.
    • During this exercise, shape of Earth in this part of globe was determined and Everest Ellipsoid was defined.
    • Survey of India redefined NGRF with reference to Earth centric WGS84 ellipsoid, in 2009.
    • For which more than 2,500 Ground control points (GCP) were established across the country.
  • Map Projections
    • A map projection is a set of mathematical transformations, which are used to represent the Earth’s curved surface on a flat map.
    • Projection Property
      • Equidistant
      • Conformal or Orthomorphic
      • Equivalent or Equal area
    • Map
      • Map is a graphical representation of the Earth surface on a 2-dimensional plane surface i.e. paper.
      • Every map will have a scale . Scales are mainly classified into 3 types.
      • Engineer’s scale E.g: 1cm= 40 m
      • Graphical Scale:
        :
      • Representative fractional scale E.g: 1 : 4000

Tools Used In Land Surveying

As mentioned in the beginning, Land surveying involves the precise measurement and mapping of land to create an accurate representation of a property or area. Hence, to achieve the accuracy and precision in the surveying process, there are many tools used for measuring the land. These tools can be separated as:

  • Legacy/Traditional Surveying Tools
  • Modern Surveying Tools

Surveying technologies are used to measure, map, and analyze the physical world, creating accurate representations of land, buildings, and other infrastructure. The evolution of surveying technologies has seen a dramatic shift from manual, labor-intensive methods to highly automated, precise, and digital technolgical systems.

Each and every tools has their own Pro's and Cons, the judgement of choosing the neccessary tools for a particular survey job mostly rely on few factors as metioned below:

  • Type of terrains where survey needs to be done
  • Total area needed to be covered under the survey
  • Time constraints
  • Budget constraints
  • Skilled manpower availability for driving the survey based on the selected surveying technology
As the above mentioned scenarios occur, a surveyor might choose one type of surveying tools or mix of several types together to expedite the work more fastly. Following are the types of Surveying tools:

  • Traditional Surveying Tools:
    Chain Surveying :
    • Pro : Simple, inexpensive, and quick for small areas.
    • Con : Limited accuracy, affected by terrain, requires manual labour, and not suitable for large areas.
    Compass Surveying :
    • Pro : Suitable for rough terrain, easy to carry and use.
    • Con : Low accuracy, affected by magnetic interference, and less reliable for large-scale mapping.
    Plane Table Surveying :
    • Pro : Good for rough areas, quick for mapping small regions.
    • Con : Time-consuming for large areas, requires a skilled operator, and accuracy depends on the operator's skill.
    Theodolite Surveying :
    • Pro : High accuracy in measuring angles, more precise than earlier methods.
    • Con : Requires a steady hand and experience, prone to errors if not handled carefully.
    Tacheometry Surveying :
    • Pro : Faster than traditional methods, suitable for rough terrain and large areas.
    • Con : Requires specialized equipment and training, lower accuracy than modern techniques.
  • Modern Surveying Tools
    Photogrammetry Surveying :
    • Pro : Can survey large areas quickly, effective for mapping inaccessible areas using aerial photos.
    • Con : Expensive, requires skilled operators and specialized equipment
    GPS/GNSS Surveying :
    • Pro : High accuracy, works globally, fast, and requires minimal human intervention.
    • Con : Expensive equipment, affected by signal obstructions (e.g., tall buildings or trees), Not suitable for detail survey.
    Electronic Total Station (ETS) :
    • Pro : Provides highly accurate measurements of both angles and distances, integrates with digital systems for easier data processing.
    • Con : Expensive, requires regular calibration, and is affected by line-of-sight obstructions.
    LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) :
    • Pro : Highly accurate, can capture vast amounts of data in 3D, effective in challenging terrain and for topographic surveys.
    • Con : Expensive equipment, data processing can be complex and time-consuming.
    UAV (Drone) Surveying :
    • Pro : Can cover small size areas quickly, provides high-resolution imagery, and is cost-effective for some applications.
    • Con : Limited by flight time and weather conditions, requires regulatory approvals in certain areas.
    Remote Sensing Surveying :
    • Pro : Can capture data over wide areas, useful for monitoring and mapping land cover, vegetation, and environmental changes. It’s non-intrusive and can access difficult-to-reach areas.
    • Con : Expensive equipment, and lack of fine resolution data that needed for Cadastral Surveying and large-scale detailed surveying.
    Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) GNSS Surveying :
    • Pro : Provides centimeter-level accuracy, ideal for precision surveying in the field.
    • Con : Expensive, requires a base station, and limited by signal obstructions and satellite availability.
    Network Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) GNSS Surveying :
    • Pro : No need of base station, provides centimeter-level accuracy, ideal for precision surveying in the field.
    • Con : Expensive, requires network of base stations, and limited by signal obstructions.
    Network Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) GNSS Surveying :
    • Pro : No need of base station, provides centimeter-level accuracy, ideal for precision surveying in the field.
    • Con : Expensive, requires network of base stations, and limited by signal obstructions.

Welcome to Chapter 2 : Survey of India’s CORS Service

Survey of India's CORS service which helps to generate high accuracy and precise points using rovers and NRTK system.

Introduction

CORS (Continuously Operating Reference Station) is a NRTK system of permanent, high-precision GNSS stations operated by Survey of India that continuously collect data and are used to provide real-time corrections for other GNSS receivers in India.

Key Features of CORS

  • Datum: National Geodetic Reference Frame (NGRF)
  • High-Precision GNSS Data
  • Real-Time Corrections
  • Centimeter-level accuracy
  • Pan India Coverage Area
